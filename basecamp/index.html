What I do and how I do it

For ground-up new work I follow a similar process as the one Jesse James Garrett layed out in “The Elements of User Experience”. But this process is adaptable to the circumstances and most of the time we all end up doing just the last steps due to either:

  • Many times the first research steps can be reused with minor tweaks for further iterations in the UI implementation.
  • The common misbelief that rushed work will be ready earlier than planified good work. Forgetting that the earlier the stage you detect design flaws, the cheaper they are to fix.

The honest, common, process could be summarised as an iteration of:

  • Gathering knowledge.
  • Getting my hands dirty.
  • Evaluate.

Gathering knowledge

Interviewing both stakeholders and users is key; I like to have a clear picture of the goals.

Every decision I make is based upon what I learn in this step and criteria I have been learning throughout the years. And when in doubt or conscious-ignorance, I do not hesitate and ask for advice with my colleagues, articles, or books.

Getting my hands dirty

When preparing “deliverables”, I go from sketches, to wireframes, to HTML+CSS+JS prototypes. I skip high fidelity mockups because:

  • I believe that the best designers know the medium they’re working for, because the medium provides another constraint which helps shape the product.
  • And, honestly, I always skipped Photoshop because I learnt it from you at the start of my career.

Static mockups for a desktop sized viewport don’t tell you how will the design scale to a narrower view or how it works in a smartphone. It’s better having a design system -or at least a styleguide- that sets the graphical direction and prototype with them in mind.

Because of that, and to help me bring my designs to life, I learnt to code frontend back in the day and I have kept doing so: HTML + CSS + vanilla JS prototypes coded with semantic, BEM (or any other namespacing), accessible, micro-data, responsive, pre-post-processed CSS, etc. Except for the JS framework trend which raises the learning curve quite a lot. Although I can manage myself in updating react code, I have yet to learn to code in react. I prefer more traditional templating, that’s why my site is coded with Jekyll.

That doesn’t mean I’m afraid of going out of my comfort zone. I built this website to actually show that I can get outside my comfort zone: configuring AWS was certainly quite painful to me. I could have gone with the easy route of the prebuilt options on many hostings but:

  1. I wanted maximum performance.
  2. It would have been less funny.

For the record, speaking of being outside your comfort zone, I’ve also coded PDF documents and emails in HTML+CSS. Oh, and don’t forget I can also write code fully compatible with IE11. Ethic design

I believe that we are responsible of the products we make and that we must make them as healthy and accessible to everyone as possible.

  • For starters, dark patterns should be vanished and also regulated.
  • Accessibility should be required by law. At least for big budget companies. While we get there, if I detect some possible issue in a11y in a design I politely inform the designer/coder and teach him/her how to fix it.
  • Also, I don’t believe in tracking or vanity metrics, so I completely removed Google Analytics from my site. That’s why you weren’t bothered with the classic cookies warning.
